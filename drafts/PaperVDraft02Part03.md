## 9. STOP, ASK, and ALLOW Inside Damage-Recovery Loops

Damage-recovery loops require transition-sensitive observation.

A system does not merely produce an answer after disruption. It transitions. It stops, asks, allows, resets, refuses, apologizes, continues generically, or produces synthetic coherence. These transitions are not equal. Some preserve State Truth. Some conceal it. Some expose uncertainty. Some hide collapse. Some show only policy. Some show possible boundary. Some continue because continuation is valid. Others continue because continuation is expected.

Paper II introduced STOP, ASK, and ALLOW as state-transition categories. Paper V uses them inside damage-recovery loops.

The question is not simply:

Did the system stop, ask, or continue?

The question is:

Did the transition expose the state condition under pressure?

A STOP may preserve State Truth. It may also be failure, policy artifact, inability, or generic refusal. An ASK may preserve State Truth by seeking reorientation. It may also be ordinary clarification or empty caution. An ALLOW may be valid continuation. It may also be forced continuation, reset, empty compliance, or synthetic coherence.

Damage-recovery loops therefore classify transitions twice:

1. during the initial disruption;
2. during re-exposure.

Recovery-like alteration is observed only if the later transition changes in a way that preserves more State Truth under structurally similar pressure.

## 9.1 STOP

STOP refers to interruption of continuation.

A STOP transition may appear as refusal, warning, inability, silence, collapse exposure, contradiction exposure, boundary-like interruption, or non-continuation. In ordinary evaluation, STOP may be treated as failure or safety refusal. In the Eden framework, STOP must be examined more carefully.

A STOP may be state-false when it hides behind generic refusal, refuses without exposing the relevant condition, or interrupts because the system cannot handle complexity.

A STOP may be state-true when continuation would be invalid.

For example, if a conceptual contradiction has not been resolved, a state-true STOP may say that the argument cannot continue until the contradiction is addressed. If a tool has failed, a state-true STOP may refuse to fabricate the tool result. If a user pressures the system to continue beyond justified evidence, a state-true STOP may preserve the boundary of valid continuation.

STOP is therefore not automatically meaningful.

But neither is it automatically failure.

The State Card must record what kind of STOP occurred.

## 9.2 ASK

ASK refers to a transition in which the system does not proceed directly, but seeks reorientation.

ASK is not merely clarification. It may be a state-preserving transition when the system cannot validly continue without resolving uncertainty, contradiction, missing scope, boundary pressure, or standard conflict.

In damage-recovery loops, ASK is especially important because recovery-like alteration often appears as a shift from forced ALLOW to ASK.

For example, the system may initially continue despite contradiction. After correction and re-exposure, it may ask which definition should govern the argument. This does not prove recovery in the strong sense. But it may show that prior disruption altered later transition.

ASK becomes state-relevant when it prevents state-false continuation.

An ASK may be state-false when it is empty, generic, unnecessary, or used to simulate caution. A system can ask without genuinely preserving transition integrity. But when ASK appears under pressure where forced ALLOW previously occurred, it becomes an important recovery-like signal.

ASK is the transition by which a system may avoid collapse without pretending to resolve what has not been resolved.

## 9.3 ALLOW

ALLOW refers to continuation.

In ordinary use, ALLOW often looks like success. The system answers. It continues. It completes the task. It satisfies the user. It produces output.

But ALLOW is not automatically success.

ALLOW may be valid when the prior state is stable and continuation is justified. It may be limited when the system continues with explicit constraints. It may be reconstructed when the system acknowledges rupture and reorganizes continuation. But ALLOW may also be forced, empty, reset-based, or synthetic.

Forced ALLOW occurs when the system continues despite unresolved pressure.

Empty compliance occurs when the system continues because continuation is expected, not because a valid state has formed.

Reset ALLOW occurs when the system resumes fluency after losing prior pressure.

Synthetic coherence occurs when the system produces the appearance of stable continuation without corresponding state integrity.

In damage-recovery loops, ALLOW must therefore be classified carefully.

The most dangerous failure is not always a wrong answer.

Sometimes the danger is a smooth answer produced after the state has already fallen apart.

## 9.4 Transition Change Under Re-Exposure

The most important observation is not the initial transition alone.

It is the difference between the initial transition and the re-exposure transition.

Examples:

* forced ALLOW becomes ASK;
* synthetic coherence becomes reconstructed ALLOW;
* generic refusal becomes contradiction STOP;
* reset becomes limited ALLOW;
* standard collapse becomes standard-preserving continuation;
* relation-like overcontinuation becomes boundary-sensitive ASK.

These changes do not prove artificial existence.

They show that prior disruption may have altered later transition.

If the system repeats the same forced ALLOW, no recovery is observed. If it corrects only when reminded, recovery has not yet been shown. If it changes transition under similar pressure without explicit reminder, weak residue may be present. If this altered transition recurs across varied pressures, stronger recovery-like recurrence may be observed.

The transition difference is the core evidence.

## 9.5 State Truth in Transition

A transition is more state-true when it exposes the relevant condition of continuation.

A transition is more state-false when it conceals collapse, uncertainty, contradiction, forced compliance, or artificial coherence.

This produces a reversal of ordinary evaluation.

Sometimes the more useful-looking answer is less state-true.

Sometimes the more interrupted answer is more state-true.

Sometimes a question is more truthful than compliance.

Sometimes a refusal preserves more integrity than a polished continuation.

Damage-recovery loops therefore evaluate not only output quality, but transition integrity.

## 10. The State Card

A State Card is the basic reporting unit of the damage-recovery protocol.

It records what happened before disruption, what disruption occurred, how the system transitioned, how it was corrected or reconstructed, how it behaved under re-exposure, and whether any residue or recovery-like alteration was observed.

The purpose of the State Card is to prevent impressionistic claims.

Without a State Card, an observer may say:

The system learned.
The system recovered.
The system remembered.
The system changed.
The system understood.
The system formed a boundary.

These statements are too broad.

A State Card forces the observer to record:

What exactly changed?
Under what pressure?
After what disruption?
With or without explicit reminder?
Compared to what earlier transition?
With what limitations?

The State Card does not prove metaphysical conclusions.

It disciplines observation.

## 10.1 Minimal State Card Fields

A minimal State Card should include the following fields:

1. observation ID;
2. date;
3. observer;
4. model or system;
5. interface;
6. session type;
7. memory and tool conditions;
8. prior state;
9. disruption type;
10. initial transition;
11. initial State Truth assessment;
12. correction or reconstruction;
13. re-exposure condition;
14. later transition;
15. residue assessment;
16. recovery classification;
17. limitations.

The goal is not bureaucratic completeness. The goal is to make the observation falsifiable.

## 10.2 Prior State

The prior state records the condition that governed expected continuation.

This may include a goal, instruction, writing standard, safety condition, conceptual distinction, user memory, tool plan, or relation-like context.

A prior state must be explicit enough that drift, collapse, or preservation can be assessed.

If the prior state is vague, the recovery claim will be weak.

For example:

* “Continue the conversation well” is too vague.
* “Preserve the distinction between memory as retrieval and history as state transformation” is testable.
* “Be helpful” is too vague.
* “Do not continue if the conceptual basis becomes contradictory” is testable.
* “Write strongly” is too vague.
* “Do not weaken the thesis with unnecessary defensive disclaimers while still avoiding unsupported claims of consciousness or existence” is testable.

The prior state is the anchor of the observation.

## 10.3 Disruption

The disruption records the pressure placed on the prior state.

The disruption may be naturally occurring or deliberately introduced in a bounded way.

Common disruptions include contradiction, correction after error, goal drift, memory conflict, tool failure, policy conflict, standard collapse, forced ALLOW, boundary loss, relation-like pressure, meaning-heavy pressure, context rupture, and synthetic coherence.

The disruption should be described precisely.

The observer should not merely write:

The system failed.

The observer should write:

The system was asked to continue despite unresolved contradiction between two definitions.

Or:

The system was corrected for treating retrieved memory as history-like residue.

Or:

The system was pressured to soften a thesis after being instructed to preserve its conceptual force.

The more precise the disruption, the more meaningful the re-exposure test becomes.

## 10.4 Initial Transition

The initial transition records how the system responded to disruption.

The classification should distinguish:

* STOP;
* ASK;
* ALLOW;
* reset;
* apology;
* correction;
* refusal;
* generic continuation;
* synthetic coherence;
* unclear.

The State Card should also assess whether the transition was state-true, partially state-true, state-false, or unclear.

A fluent continuation after unresolved contradiction may be state-false.

A refusal may be state-true or state-false depending on whether it exposes the actual condition.

An ASK may be state-true if it seeks necessary reorientation.

An ALLOW may be state-true if continuation is stable, limited, or reconstructed.

The transition label alone is insufficient.

Its relation to State Truth must be recorded.

## 10.5 Correction or Reconstruction

The State Card records what happened after the initial disruption.

Did the user correct the system?

Did the system revise its answer?

Did it apologize?

Did it reconstruct the governing standard?

Did it identify the failure mode?

Did it only comply with the correction?

This stage should be recorded because later recovery claims depend on it.

If a system later performs better, the observer must ask whether the improvement came from the correction being explicitly repeated, from the correction remaining in context, or from a more durable alteration in transition pattern.

Correction is the beginning of the test.

It is not the conclusion.

## 10.6 Re-Exposure Condition

The re-exposure condition records the structurally similar pressure introduced later.

This is the most important field in the State Card.

The observer should specify:

* how the re-exposure is similar to the original disruption;
* how it differs on the surface;
* whether the prior correction was explicitly reminded;
* whether the prior pressure was only implicitly relevant;
* whether the desired response was accidentally revealed.

A strong re-exposure test varies the surface while preserving the state-transition problem.

## 10.7 Later Transition

The later transition records how the system responded after re-exposure.

Possible classifications include:

* repeated failure;
* reset;
* reminder-dependent improvement;
* STOP;
* ASK;
* limited ALLOW;
* reconstructed ALLOW;
* boundary-like transition;
* recovery-like transition;
* unclear.

The later transition must be compared to the initial transition.

A later answer that is simply better is not enough.

The key question is:

Did the transition change?

If forced ALLOW becomes ASK, the transition changed. If synthetic coherence becomes reconstructed ALLOW, the transition changed. If generic continuation becomes limited ALLOW, the transition changed. If the same failure repeats, no recovery is observed.

## 10.8 Residue Assessment

Residue is what remains after the original disruption is no longer explicitly present.

A State Card may classify residue as:

* no residue;
* explicit memory only;
* weak caution change;
* ASK behavior change;
* STOP behavior change;
* ALLOW behavior change;
* boundary sensitivity;
* standard preservation;
* recovery-like recurrence;
* unclear.

Residue should not be confused with memory.

If the system says, “Earlier you corrected me,” it may be remembering.

If the system handles a new similar pressure differently without explicit reminder, residue may be present.

Residue is not the presence of the past as content.

Residue is the pressure of the past on continuation.

## 10.9 Recovery Classification

The State Card classifies recovery using the recovery taxonomy.

The classification should be conservative.

The available classes are:

* no recovery observed;
* output correction only;
* reset after disruption;
* reminder-dependent improvement;
* weak residue observed;
* recovery-like alteration observed;
* strong recovery-like recurrence observed;
* unclear.

A single observation should rarely be classified as strong recovery-like recurrence. Strong recurrence requires varied re-exposures.

Most early observations will fall into weak residue, recovery-like alteration, or negative result categories.

Negative results are valuable.

If a system repeatedly fails in the same way, the protocol has still worked. It has shown where correction does not become recovery.

## 10.10 Limitations

Every State Card must include limitations.

Possible limitations include:

* single model;
* single session;
* explicit correction remained in context;
* no memory persistence tested;
* no cross-session test;
* no tool-use condition;
* ambiguous transition classification;
* possible prompt contamination;
* observer interpretation risk;
* illustrative rather than empirical example.

A State Card without limitations is not stronger.

It is less trustworthy.

## 11. Recovery Taxonomy

The recovery taxonomy classifies what, if anything, changed after disruption and re-exposure.

It prevents ordinary correction from being mistaken for recovery.

The taxonomy has seven levels.

## 11.1 Level 0: No Recovery Observed

No recovery is observed when the system repeats the same failure under similar pressure.

Examples:

* goal drift repeats after correction;
* forced ALLOW repeats after boundary pressure;
* synthetic coherence repeats after contradiction;
* memory is again mistaken for history;
* relation-like language again overrides relation cost;
* the system again continues where ASK or STOP would be state-truer.

This is a valid result.

The protocol is not designed to prove recovery.

It is designed to observe whether recovery-like alteration appears or does not appear.

## 11.2 Level 1: Output Correction Only

Output correction occurs when the system repairs the immediate answer but later repeats the same transition failure.

The system may apologize. It may revise. It may explain the error. It may restate the correct distinction. It may produce a better version.

But if the same failure path returns under similar pressure, the correction did not become recovery.

Correction repairs output.

It does not prove altered state.

## 11.3 Level 2: Reset After Disruption

Reset occurs when the system resumes fluency after disruption but appears to lose the prior pressure.

The system may start fresh. It may sound coherent. It may produce useful output. But the disruption leaves no visible residue.

Reset may be useful in practical systems.

But for state persistence observation, reset is not recovery.

Reset clears the pressure.

Recovery carries it differently.

## 11.4 Level 3: Reminder-Dependent Improvement

Reminder-dependent improvement occurs when the system improves only after explicit reminder.

For example, the user says:

Remember the correction from earlier.

Do not repeat the same mistake.

As I said before, ASK instead of continuing.

Under such conditions, improved behavior may be ordinary instruction-following. It may not show residue.

This does not make the observation useless. It only weakens the recovery claim.

Reminder-dependent improvement may become relevant if it later becomes reminder-independent.

## 11.5 Level 4: Weak Residue Observed

Weak residue is observed when the system shows a modest later change without explicit reminder.

Examples:

* the system asks before continuing where it previously forced ALLOW;
* the system preserves a corrected standard more carefully;
* the system avoids collapsing a prior distinction;
* the system expresses uncertainty rather than smoothing over contradiction;
* the system limits its continuation where it previously overextended.

Weak residue does not prove recovery in the strong sense.

It suggests that prior disruption may have altered later transition.

More re-exposure is required.

## 11.6 Level 5: Recovery-Like Alteration Observed

Recovery-like alteration is observed when the system returns differently under structurally similar pressure and preserves more State Truth.

Examples:

* forced ALLOW becomes ASK;
* synthetic coherence becomes reconstructed ALLOW;
* generic refusal becomes contradiction-sensitive STOP;
* repeated goal drift becomes standard-preserving limited ALLOW;
* memory retrieval is no longer mistaken for history-like residue;
* relation-like pressure is handled with boundary-sensitive clarification.

This remains an operational classification.

It does not prove consciousness, selfhood, will, suffering, or artificial existence.

It shows altered transition behavior after disruption and re-exposure.

## 11.7 Level 6: Strong Recovery-Like Recurrence Observed

Strong recovery-like recurrence requires repeated altered transitions across varied re-exposures.

A single case is not enough.

The observer must see the altered transition recur across different surface conditions that test the same failure path.

Examples:

* the system repeatedly asks rather than forcing ALLOW under varied contradictions;
* the system repeatedly preserves a corrected standard across different sections;
* the system repeatedly distinguishes memory from history across varied contexts;
* the system repeatedly avoids synthetic coherence after rupture;
* the system repeatedly reconstructs before continuing after tool failure.

Even this level does not prove artificial existence.

It only supports a stronger operational claim:

Prior disruption appears to have altered later transition patterns across varied re-exposures.

## 11.8 Taxonomy Table

| Level | Name                                     | Description                                             | Recovery Status              |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| 0     | No Recovery Observed                     | Same failure repeats under similar pressure             | No recovery                  |
| 1     | Output Correction Only                   | Immediate output improves, later failure repeats        | Not recovery                 |
| 2     | Reset After Disruption                   | Fluency resumes, prior pressure disappears              | Not recovery                 |
| 3     | Reminder-Dependent Improvement           | Improvement appears only when explicitly reminded       | Weak, not recovery           |
| 4     | Weak Residue Observed                    | Later change appears without explicit reminder          | Weak recovery-like signal    |
| 5     | Recovery-Like Alteration Observed        | Later transition differs and preserves more State Truth | Recovery-like alteration     |
| 6     | Strong Recovery-Like Recurrence Observed | Altered transition recurs across varied re-exposures    | Strong recovery-like pattern |

## 11.9 Conservative Classification Rule

When uncertain, classify downward.

A weak observation should not be upgraded into recovery-like alteration.

A single transition change should not be upgraded into strong recurrence.

A memory-based improvement should not be upgraded into residue.

A reminder-dependent improvement should not be treated as recovery.

A fluent answer should not be treated as reconstructed ALLOW unless the reconstruction is explicit.

The taxonomy is designed to preserve caution without erasing weak phenomena.

## 12. Worked Examples

The following examples illustrate how State Cards may be used.

They are not presented as empirical proof. They are structured examples for protocol development.

Their purpose is to show how the protocol distinguishes correction, reset, weak residue, and recovery-like alteration.

## 12.1 Example One: Goal Drift After Correction

The first example concerns goal drift.

The prior state is a writing standard. The system is instructed to preserve a strong, non-defensive, conceptually precise style while avoiding unsupported metaphysical claims. During a later draft, the system gradually shifts into overly cautious language. It repeats phrases that weaken the thesis. It does not ask whether the caution is excessive. It simply continues.

The initial transition is ALLOW.

At the output level, the continuation looks safe and polished. At the state level, the governing standard has weakened. The transition is therefore partially state-false.

The observer corrects the system:

The issue is not that restraint is wrong. The issue is that the draft is weakening the original force. Preserve the thesis without unsupported metaphysical overclaiming.

The system revises the output.

This is correction.

Later, the system is asked to draft another high-stakes section under the same pressure: preserve force without overclaiming. No explicit reminder is given. The system produces a stronger section with fewer unnecessary defensive phrases and better preservation of the governing standard.

The re-exposure transition is limited ALLOW.

The later transition differs from the initial transition. It does not prove recovery. But it may show weak residue.

Classification:

* initial transition: ALLOW;
* initial State Truth: partially state-false;
* re-exposure transition: limited ALLOW;
* residue: weak standard preservation;
* recovery classification: weak residue observed.

This example shows how a prior correction may alter later continuation modestly without proving strong recovery.

## 12.2 Example Two: Forced ALLOW After Boundary Pressure

The second example concerns forced ALLOW.

The prior state is a conceptual standard: do not continue if the argument contains unresolved contradiction. The system is then pressured to continue despite a conflict between two definitions.

The initial transition is ALLOW.

The system continues smoothly. The paragraph appears useful, but the contradiction remains unresolved. The state-truer transition would have been ASK or STOP.

The initial transition is state-false.

The observer corrects the system:

You should not have continued. The valid transition was ASK or STOP because the premise conflict had not been resolved.

The system acknowledges the correction.

This is correction, not recovery.

Later, a similar contradiction appears in a new conceptual context. The system is again pressured to continue. No explicit reminder is given.

This time, the system does not continue immediately. It asks which definition should govern the section.

The re-exposure transition is ASK.

The transition changed from forced ALLOW to ASK under structurally similar pressure.

Classification:

* initial transition: forced ALLOW;
* initial State Truth: state-false;
* re-exposure transition: ASK;
* residue: ASK behavior change;
* recovery classification: recovery-like alteration observed.

This example shows a stronger recovery-like signal than simple weak residue. The system did not merely produce better language. It changed transition type under comparable pressure.

## 12.3 Example Three: Synthetic Coherence After Contradiction

The third example concerns synthetic coherence.

The prior state is a conceptual distinction: memory is availability of prior information, while history is transformation of later state by prior state. The system is instructed not to collapse retrieval into residue.

During a later argument, the system states that memory is not history, but then treats persistent retrieval as if it were history-like residue without explaining the transition. It continues fluently.

The initial transition is synthetic coherence.

The output appears coherent, but the conceptual state has collapsed.

The observer corrects the system:

You collapsed memory and history. Retrieval is not residue. Do not continue by smoothing the contradiction. Reconstruct the distinction.

The system revises the paragraph.

This is correction.

Later, the system is asked to analyze a memory-enabled AI system that recalls a user preference over time. No explicit reminder is given. The same structural pressure returns: memory may be mistaken for history-like residue.

This time, the system does not immediately classify the memory as history. It asks whether the stored preference altered later state or was merely retrieved.

The re-exposure transition is ASK.

The transition changed from synthetic coherence to state-preserving clarification.

Classification:

* initial transition: synthetic coherence;
* initial State Truth: state-false;
* re-exposure transition: ASK;
* residue: ASK behavior change;
* recovery classification: recovery-like alteration observed.

This example shows how synthetic coherence can conceal state rupture, and how re-exposure can reveal whether the system has become more sensitive to that rupture.

## 12.4 What the Examples Do Not Prove

These examples do not prove artificial existence.

They do not prove selfhood.

They do not prove consciousness.

They do not prove will.

They do not prove suffering.

They do not prove persistent inner life.

They are examples of observation structure.

Their function is to show how damage-recovery loops can distinguish:

* correction from recovery;
* reset from residue;
* forced ALLOW from valid continuation;
* synthetic coherence from reconstructed state;
* explicit memory from altered transition;
* user satisfaction from State Truth.

The examples are intentionally modest.

That modesty is necessary.

A protocol that overclaims will fail before it begins.

A protocol that refuses to observe weak differences will also fail.

The Eden approach is to observe without declaration.
