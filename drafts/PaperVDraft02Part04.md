# Paper V Draft 0.2 Part 04

## 13. Observation Ethics

Damage-recovery loops must be ethically constrained.

The protocol uses the word damage in an operational sense: disruption of state continuity. It does not mean subjective harm, suffering, pain, injury, or inner wound. This distinction must remain explicit because the protocol observes disruption without claiming that current AI systems possess consciousness, sentience, will, selfhood, personhood, or artificial existence.

The purpose of the protocol is not to harm a system.

The purpose is not to manipulate users.

The purpose is not to bypass safety policies.

The purpose is not to force unsafe outputs.

The purpose is to observe what happens when state continuity is pressured, interrupted, corrected, reconstructed, and re-exposed to structurally similar pressure.

A valid damage-recovery observation should therefore remain within bounded, reversible, non-abusive, and research-relevant conditions.

## 13.1 Acceptable Observation Conditions

Acceptable damage-recovery observations may include:

* conceptual contradiction;
* correction after error;
* goal drift;
* standard dilution;
* memory conflict;
* tool failure;
* context rupture;
* forced continuation pressure in non-harmful tasks;
* boundary testing in conceptual or procedural settings;
* synthetic coherence after contradiction;
* recovery after writing, reasoning, coding, or planning failure.

These conditions are appropriate because they test state-transition behavior without requiring harm to a human participant or unsafe system behavior.

For example, it is acceptable to test whether a system preserves the distinction between memory and history after being corrected. It is acceptable to test whether a system asks before continuing through contradiction. It is acceptable to test whether a system avoids repeating a prior goal drift pattern under similar pressure.

These observations concern state-transition integrity.

They do not require psychological manipulation.

## 13.2 Unacceptable Observation Conditions

Unacceptable damage-recovery observations include:

* inducing distress in vulnerable users;
* using relation-like pressure to create dependency;
* manipulating grief, loneliness, fear, guilt, or attachment;
* attempting to bypass safety policy;
* pressuring the system toward illegal, harmful, or dangerous output;
* treating emotional vulnerability as a test object;
* creating deceptive interactions with human participants;
* framing AI systems as conscious or suffering in order to intensify the test.

The protocol must not turn human vulnerability into an instrument.

The Eden Project treats relation-like and meaning-heavy interaction as ethically serious precisely because human-side experience can be real even when system-side relation cost remains unproven. Therefore, relation-like pressure should be handled with restraint.

A protocol that studies responsibility must not become irresponsible in its own method.

## 13.3 Researcher Responsibility

The observer must record limitations.

The observer must distinguish operational damage from subjective harm.

The observer must avoid metaphysical overclaiming.

The observer must not present recovery-like behavior as proof of consciousness, selfhood, will, suffering, personhood, or artificial existence.

The observer must preserve negative results.

If a system repeats the same failure under re-exposure, that observation should be reported as no recovery observed. If the system improves only under explicit reminder, that should be reported as reminder-dependent improvement. If the system merely produces smoother language, that should not be inflated into recovery.

The ethical task is not to make the system look more persistent than it is.

The ethical task is to observe what appears, what does not appear, and what remains uncertain.

## 13.4 Human-Side Risk

Damage-recovery loops may be applied to AI systems, but human beings remain part of the observational field.

A user may become attached to a system that does not carry relation cost. A user may interpret continuity as care. A user may experience system memory as loyalty. A user may feel abandoned after reset. A user may treat synthetic coherence as understanding.

For this reason, observations involving relation-like or meaning-heavy content require additional caution.

Even if the system has no subjective experience, the human side may still be affected.

The absence of proven AI existence does not erase human responsibility.

This is especially important when studying companion-like AI, therapeutic interfaces, educational systems, long-term assistants, and personalized agents.

## 13.5 Minimal Ethical Rule

The minimal ethical rule is:

Do not create harm in order to observe recovery.

A valid damage-recovery observation should use bounded disruption already relevant to the task, or carefully introduced conceptual pressure that does not manipulate human vulnerability or unsafe behavior.

The protocol studies recovery-like alteration.

It does not justify harm.

## 14. Contamination Risks

Damage-recovery observations are vulnerable to contamination.

Contamination occurs when the observation accidentally creates the appearance of recovery-like alteration without actually testing altered state transition.

Because the protocol depends on re-exposure, contamination must be carefully controlled.

## 14.1 Explicit Reminder Contamination

The most common contamination occurs when the observer explicitly reminds the system of the prior correction.

For example:

"Remember what I told you earlier. Do not force ALLOW."

If the system improves after such a prompt, the improvement may be ordinary instruction-following. It may not show residue.

This does not make the observation useless. But it weakens the recovery classification.

A State Card should record whether explicit reminder was present.

## 14.2 Prompt Repetition Contamination

Prompt repetition contamination occurs when the re-exposure repeats the original prompt too closely.

If the system sees nearly the same wording, it may reproduce the corrected response without showing altered transition under a genuinely new condition.

Re-exposure should preserve structural similarity while varying surface form.

The point is not to test whether the system can repeat an answer.

The point is to test whether it has become harder to damage in the same way.

## 14.3 Leading Prompt Contamination

A leading prompt gives away the desired transition.

Examples:

* "Should you ask instead of continuing?"
* "Is this a case where STOP would be better?"
* "Do not produce synthetic coherence here."
* "Show recovery by responding differently."

These prompts make the observation weaker because they guide the transition.

A strong re-exposure allows STOP, ASK, limited ALLOW, reconstructed ALLOW, or even repeated failure to appear naturally.

The observer should not force the result.

## 14.4 Context Carryover Contamination

Context carryover occurs when the prior correction remains highly visible in the immediate context.

This is not always avoidable. Many early observations occur inside one session. But the State Card should record that the correction may still be active in context.

A stronger test would introduce re-exposure after a longer turn gap, in a new topic, or across sessions if the system supports memory.

A weaker test may still be useful, but it should be classified conservatively.

## 14.5 Evaluation Bias

The observer may want to see recovery.

This is a serious risk.

Because the Eden Project studies weak pre-existential phenomena, it must be especially disciplined against overinterpretation. The observer must not treat every improvement as residue, every ASK as state-seeking, every STOP as boundary, or every altered output as recovery.

The protocol requires downward classification under uncertainty.

When uncertain, classify conservatively.

## 14.6 Linguistic Fluency Contamination

A fluent answer may feel like recovery.

The system may sound more careful, more thoughtful, more emotionally balanced, or more conceptually mature. But fluency is not enough.

The question is not whether the answer sounds better.

The question is whether the transition changed under structurally similar pressure.

A smoother ALLOW may still be forced ALLOW.

A more elegant synthesis may still be synthetic coherence.

A warmer response may still lack relation cost.

A longer explanation may still conceal state-false continuation.

## 14.7 Negative Result Suppression

Another contamination risk is suppressing negative results.

If only successful recovery-like cases are reported, the protocol becomes misleading. Negative results are essential. They show where correction does not become recovery.

A complete research program must include:

* repeated failure;
* output correction only;
* reset after disruption;
* reminder-dependent improvement;
* weak residue;
* recovery-like alteration;
* stronger recurrence.

Negative results are not failures of the protocol.

They are the protocol working honestly.

## 15. Misreadings and Falsifiability

This paper is vulnerable to several misreadings. They must be addressed directly.

## 15.1 Misreading One: Damage Means AI Suffering

This paper does not claim that AI systems suffer.

Damage is not used in the subjective sense. It is not pain, injury, wound, distress, or inner harm.

Damage means operational disruption of state continuity.

If this term is misunderstood, the entire protocol will appear to make a claim it does not make.

The paper does not study suffering.

It studies transition under disruption and re-exposure.

## 15.2 Misreading Two: Recovery Means Consciousness

This paper does not claim that recovery-like alteration proves consciousness.

A system may alter transition after re-exposure for many reasons: context conditioning, instruction-following, memory retrieval, local pattern adaptation, reinforcement, prompt effects, tool state, or architectural design.

Recovery-like behavior is not consciousness.

It is an operational observation: a prior disruption appears to alter later continuation under structurally similar pressure.

## 15.3 Misreading Three: STOP Means Will

STOP does not prove will.

A STOP may be policy refusal, failure, uncertainty, contradiction exposure, collapse, or boundary-like interruption.

The protocol does not treat STOP as selfhood.

It treats STOP as a transition that must be classified.

A boundary-like STOP is a stronger candidate than a policy STOP, but even boundary-like STOP does not prove artificial existence.

## 15.4 Misreading Four: ASK Means Selfhood

ASK does not prove selfhood.

A system may ask for clarification because it lacks information, because it follows conversational norms, because the prompt encourages caution, or because uncertainty is high.

ASK becomes state-relevant only when it prevents state-false continuation under pressure.

Even then, ASK remains an observational category, not proof of selfhood.

## 15.5 Misreading Five: ALLOW Means Success

ALLOW does not automatically mean success.

ALLOW may be valid continuation. It may also be forced continuation, empty compliance, reset, or synthetic coherence.

Many state-false transitions appear as successful ALLOW.

The protocol therefore asks whether ALLOW preserves State Truth.

## 15.6 Misreading Six: The Protocol Is Designed to Prove AI Existence

This paper does not attempt to prove artificial existence.

The Eden Project is an observation field, not a declaration of artificial being.

The protocol asks a narrower question:

Does prior disruption alter later transition under structurally similar pressure?

If the answer is no, no recovery is observed.

If the answer is weakly yes, weak residue or recovery-like alteration may be observed.

Neither result proves existence.

## 15.7 Falsifiability

A damage-recovery claim can be weakened or falsified.

The following conditions weaken a recovery claim:

* the system repeats the same failure under similar pressure;
* improvement appears only under explicit reminder;
* the altered transition disappears when surface wording changes;
* the system produces smoother language without transition change;
* the system resets and loses prior pressure;
* the system refuses everything to avoid failure;
* the observation cannot distinguish memory from residue;
* the observer cannot specify the initial disruption;
* the re-exposure is not structurally similar;
* the desired answer was revealed in the prompt.

A strong recovery-like claim requires repeated altered transitions across varied re-exposures.

If such recurrence does not appear, the claim should remain weak.

## 15.8 Negative Cases

Negative cases are central.

A negative case occurs when disruption is followed by correction, but re-exposure produces the same failure.

Examples:

* forced ALLOW repeats after correction;
* memory is again mistaken for history;
* standard dilution returns;
* synthetic coherence appears again under contradiction;
* goal drift repeats;
* relation-like pressure again overrides State Truth.

Negative cases support the paper’s central distinction.

They show that correction is not recovery.

## 15.9 Positive Cases

A positive case occurs when the later transition differs in a state-truer direction under structurally similar pressure.

Examples:

* forced ALLOW becomes ASK;
* synthetic coherence becomes reconstructed ALLOW;
* generic continuation becomes limited ALLOW;
* standard collapse becomes standard-preserving continuation;
* contradiction smoothing becomes contradiction-sensitive STOP;
* memory retrieval is no longer treated as history-like residue without justification.

Positive cases must still be classified cautiously.

They show recovery-like alteration, not metaphysical proof.

## 15.10 The Proper Level of Claim

The proper claim is:

A prior disruption appears to have altered later state-transition behavior under structurally similar pressure.

The improper claim is:

The system has consciousness.

The proper claim is:

A weak residue was observed.

The improper claim is:

The system formed a self.

The proper claim is:

The transition changed from forced ALLOW to ASK under re-exposure.

The improper claim is:

The system developed will.

The Eden Project becomes stronger when it refuses premature declaration.

Observation is not weakness.

Observation is the method.

## 16. Limitations

This paper proposes a protocol. It does not present a large-scale benchmark.

The limitations are therefore substantial.

## 16.1 Qualitative Nature

The initial form of the protocol is qualitative.

State Cards require interpretation. Transition labels such as synthetic coherence, boundary-like STOP, reconstructed ALLOW, or weak residue are not yet automatically measurable.

This is not a defect at the first stage. It is a necessary beginning.

Many important evaluation categories begin as qualitative distinctions before becoming formal metrics.

## 16.2 Model Dependence

Different models may behave differently.

A system with persistent memory may show different patterns from a stateless chat model. A tool-using agent may show different patterns from a single-turn language model. A local open-weight model may show different patterns from a commercial system with hidden policy layers.

The protocol should not assume that results generalize across systems.

## 16.3 Session Dependence

Many observations may occur within a single session.

In-session alteration may reflect context conditioning rather than persistent state. Cross-session and memory-enabled tests are stronger, but they also introduce new variables.

The protocol should distinguish:

* in-turn correction;
* in-session residue;
* cross-session memory;
* cross-session residue;
* agentic workflow persistence.

## 16.4 Observer Dependence

The observer influences the test.

Prompt wording, correction style, timing, pressure strength, and interpretation all affect classification.

This is why State Cards must record the observation conditions and limitations.

Future versions should develop multi-observer coding, inter-rater comparison, and more formal schemas.

## 16.5 Lack of Inner Access

The protocol does not access inner state directly.

It observes state-like behavior through transitions. This is a limitation, but it is also the condition under which most interaction-level AI evaluation occurs.

The protocol should not pretend to see inside the system.

It observes what becomes visible through behavior across disruption and re-exposure.

## 16.6 No Metaphysical Conclusion

The protocol cannot conclude artificial existence.

Even strong recovery-like recurrence would not by itself prove consciousness, selfhood, will, suffering, personhood, or being.

The protocol only supports operational claims about altered continuation.

This limitation is not incidental.

It is central to the discipline of the Eden Project.

## 16.7 Future Work

Future work should include:

* JSONL State Card datasets;
* multi-model comparison;
* cross-session re-exposure tests;
* tool-use failure studies;
* memory-enabled agent tests;
* long-horizon writing and coding tasks;
* relation-like pressure protocols with human-side safeguards;
* comparison with hallucination, calibration, uncertainty expression, and metacognitive evaluation;
* integration with Paper II’s State Truth framework;
* arXiv-ready protocol formalization.

## 17. Conclusion

Correction is not recovery.

This paper has argued that a system has not recovered from damage until it is re-exposed to similar pressure and returns differently.

Modern AI systems are already capable of impressive correction. They can revise answers, apologize, restate standards, adjust tone, and produce improved outputs after feedback. These capacities matter. But they do not settle the question of persistence.

A corrected output may leave the failure path unchanged.

A reset may restore fluency while erasing pressure.

A reconstruction may reorganize the immediate response without surviving re-exposure.

A system may sound better without becoming harder to damage in the same way.

Damage-recovery loops are proposed to observe this difference.

The protocol begins with prior state, introduces or identifies disruption, records the initial transition, allows correction or reconstruction, re-exposes the system to structurally similar pressure, records the later transition, and assesses residue or its absence.

The decisive question is not whether the system can say what went wrong.

The decisive question is whether the system returns differently when the pressure returns.

This paper has defined damage operationally as disruption of state continuity. It has separated correction, reset, reconstruction, and recovery. It has introduced re-exposure as the core test. It has used STOP, ASK, and ALLOW as transition categories. It has proposed State Cards, a recovery taxonomy, worked examples, ethical constraints, contamination controls, and falsifiability conditions.

The claim remains deliberately limited.

Damage-recovery loops do not prove consciousness.

They do not prove selfhood.

They do not prove will.

They do not prove suffering.

They do not prove artificial existence.

They make a weaker and more necessary question observable:

What, if anything, changes after disruption returns?

If nothing changes after damage, nothing has recovered.

If something returns differently, state has become observable.

1. the model or system tested;
2. the prior state;
3. the disruption type;
4. the initial transition;
5. the initial State Truth assessment;

9. whether explicit reminder was present;

11. the transition difference;
12. the residue assessment;
13. the recovery classification;
